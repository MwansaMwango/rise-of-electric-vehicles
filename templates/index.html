Current Situation - Only 0.48% of the 1.5 billion vehicles are electric!

It is 2021 and yes, the Rise of Electric Vehicles is here. Howevwe, as of September, 2019, only 0.48% of the 1.5 billion vehicles in the world are electric with Europe taking the lead in embracing EVs. This is quickly changing with carbon emissions reduction presure, innovation in car manufacturing and battery technology.

EV adoption is bound to happen and we investigate what will aspects the will matter to make this a reality. The world has began to shift to electric vehicles.

Europe dominates when it comes to EV market share of all new car sales

In 2019, Europe overtook China with the proportion of electric vehicles sold. With the push for greener and cleaner solutions, major car manufacturers like Tesla, BYD, Hyundai, Volkswagen and Nissan, have all made the hurried dash for the green race.

But why the low EV market share of only 0.48%?

Its stems to the big question - Why would you buy an EV anyway right? Consumers and manufactueres still have concerns about the “Green Vehicle” market. We investigated the extent of these conerns and barriers to entry.

High Price

By far, historically, the biggest barrier to entry into the EV market has been the high cost. Because of its novelty, an EV-product such as the Leaf would landed a hefty $60,000 price tag in 2012. With that price tag, your typical consumer would be assuming it comes with the luxury of either a 4-door sedan, leather interiors or a 5 door SUV, with a horse power of a Ferrari.

What did we learn?

Due to innovation in materials and manufacturing methods, Battery costs have been declining dramatically. About 21% of lithium battery EV cost lies in the battery pack. As of December 2019, the cost of electric-vehicle batteries has fallen 87% since 2010.

Wrights Law Pioneered by Theodore Wright in 1936, Wright's Law aims to provide a reliable framework for forecasting cost declines as a function of cumulative production. Specifically, it states that for every cumulative doubling of units produced, costs will fall by a constant percentage. This decline in cost is evident in the evolution from first cars, computers or mobile phones.

Range Anxiety

Consumers fear that they will run out of 'juice' in transit and have to charge their export const if they can find a charging station nearby. Whats worse if it takes long to charge worse have to recharge frequently. Which highlights the next obvious barrier to adoption.

What did we learn?

Today, the average battery range for EVs is ranges from 350-450km, while Tesla has the Model 3 Long Range up to 657km. This is comparable to an average sized conventional car's full tank range.

Charge Station Availability

Consumers, including Australian's, are held back by not having sufficient charging facilities. Definitely, wouldn't want to ran out of 'juice' in the middle of Australian outback.

What did we learn?


There are a lot more charging stations in Europe, including the number of charging points per station. The US is catching up, but Australia is way behind. It is difficult to get information on China.

Electricity Generation Source

Well, our electricity is monopolised by the fossil fuel industry. today. While there are indeed alternatives such as solar, wind and thermal platforms. Significant portions of fossil fuel production are still being channelled back into our grid and therefore, is Man’s energy source for switching on the lights at night. It’s a sad paradox, but a true one at best.

What did we learn?

Countries who have adopted renewable source are more likely to also have more EV's on the road.

Looking at the data available from the US alone. With their giga-factories, innovations made to its batteries and car design created the benchmark for such types of vehicles supporting our road network.

What does the future hold?

With manufacturing and battery innovation, government support, competition and climate change, the rise of EVs has already began and set to explode.
